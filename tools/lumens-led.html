About this lumens to watts (LED) converter

When buying light bulbs, people often compare products by watts. That works for older technologies such as incandescent bulbs, because wattage was closely linked to brightness. With LED lighting, it’s different: LEDs can produce much more light per watt, so watts measure energy use, while lumens measure brightness. That’s why lumens are the better metric to compare how bright two lamps will look, and watts are better to estimate electricity consumption.

Converting lumens to watts is not an exact physical conversion like liters to gallons. It depends on the LED’s luminous efficacy, expressed as lumens per watt (lm/W). A high-quality LED can deliver more lumens for the same wattage than a cheaper model. Color temperature, optics, beam angle, and driver efficiency can also influence the final numbers.

This calculator uses a simple relationship: Watts ≈ Lumens ÷ (lm/W) and Lumens ≈ Watts × (lm/W). By default, it uses 90 lm/W, which is a common “average household LED” assumption, but you can change it if you know your bulb’s rated efficacy. For example, if an LED is rated around 100 lm/W, then 800 lumens would correspond to roughly 8 watts; with 80 lm/W, the same 800 lumens would be closer to 10 watts.

Everything runs locally in your browser: no upload, no server processing, and results appear instantly. Use this tool for quick comparisons and estimates, and rely on the manufacturer’s lumen output and watt rating for the most accurate values.

Tip: if you are replacing an old bulb, compare lumens for brightness, then check watts for energy consumption.

What is this lumens ↔ watts (LED) converter used for?

Common mistakes

A frequent mistake is assuming there is a fixed conversion between lumens and watts. Unlike mechanical units, this conversion depends on LED efficiency (lm/W), which varies by technology, quality, and design. Another error is comparing LEDs only by wattage instead of brightness.

Limits and assumptions

This tool provides an estimate based on an assumed luminous efficacy. Real-world values may differ depending on the LED, optics, color temperature, and driver efficiency. Always check the manufacturer’s specifications for exact ratings.

Educational summary

Lumens measure how bright a lamp appears, while watts measure how much electricity it consumes. For LED lighting, brightness per watt varies. This tool helps you understand and estimate that relationship quickly.
