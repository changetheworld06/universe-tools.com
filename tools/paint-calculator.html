Paint calculator

Estimate liters of paint from surface area, coverage (m²/L), number of coats, and optional extra. Everything runs locally in your browser.

Enter the total surface area to paint, your paint coverage (m² per liter), and how many coats you plan to apply. Optionally add an extra percentage for absorption, texture, and small losses.

About this paint calculator

Buying paint is one of those simple tasks that often leads to surprises: you either end up short and must run back to the store, or you overbuy and store half-open cans for years. This free paint calculator helps you estimate how many liters you need using a straightforward method based on surface area, paint coverage, and the number of coats. It’s useful for walls, ceilings, rooms, renovation projects, and any situation where you want a quick approximation before purchasing supplies.

The key idea is to compute the total “area to cover” by multiplying your surface area (in m²) by the number of coats. Then the tool divides that figure by the coverage rate (m² per liter) printed on your paint can. For example, if you have 40 m² to paint, plan 2 coats, and your paint covers 10 m² per liter, your baseline estimate is (40 × 2) / 10 = 8 liters. Because real-life conditions vary, you can also add an optional extra percentage for porous surfaces, textured walls, absorption, roller losses, edges, and small spills.

Coverage values differ across brands, finishes, and surface preparation. Smooth primed walls can achieve higher coverage, while raw plaster, rough texture, dark-to-light color changes, or repainting over stains may require more paint. As a general benchmark, many interior paints fall somewhere around 8–12 m²/L, but your product label is the best reference. If you are unsure, use a conservative coverage number and add a small extra percentage to reduce the risk of running short.

Everything is computed locally in your browser. No account is required and no data is sent to a server. The result is an estimate, not a guarantee—always follow the manufacturer’s instructions and consider your surface conditions.

Tip: if you are painting a whole room, measure walls and ceiling separately and add them together for the most accurate surface area.
